Restating your goal
You want to implement RPG-II targeting your COR24 emulator, and you’re asking what language RPG itself was originally implemented in—presumably to inform bootstrapping, architecture, or translation strategy.

Short Answer
RPG (including RPG-II) was primarily implemented in assembly language, often tightly coupled to the hardware and operating system of the target machine (especially IBM System/3, System/32, System/34, and System/36).

Why Assembly Was Used
Historical / Architectural Context
RPG originated in the late 1950s / early 1960s on IBM mainframes.

Early systems had:

Very limited memory (KB-scale)

No portable runtime environments

No mature high-level implementation languages

Design Implications
RPG was:

Not just a language, but a report generation system

Deeply tied to:

Card input formats

Fixed-format records

Printer output devices

Cycle-based execution model

Because of this, the compiler/interpreter:

Needed tight control over I/O

Was often closer to a domain-specific runtime system

Was written in assembly for performance and control

Evolution Over Time
Era	Implementation Language
RPG I / II (1960s–1970s)	Assembly (mostly IBM internal)
RPG III (System/38 era)	Still largely low-level, some higher-level tooling
RPG IV (ILE RPG)	Likely implemented in C/C++ or internal IBM HLLs

Thought for 20s
For an embedded COR24 RPG-II project, the practical answer is:

Most likely: assembly language / machine code
The original IBM RPG family dates back to the IBM 1401 era (1959), and RPG II arrived around 1969 for systems like the System/3. On machines of that era, language processors such as compilers were typically written in assembler or very low-level machine code, not in a higher-level language like COBOL or FORTRAN. The 1401 itself was initially programmed in machine language and Autocoder, and RPG was introduced as one of its early high-level business languages. 

For RPG II specifically, IBM documentation describes it as a compiler program for System/3 rather than an interpreter, and contemporary System/3 materials and later RPG practice strongly fit the pattern of these compilers being implemented in the machine’s own low-level systems code. 

What that means architecturally
If your real question is:

“How should I implement RPG-II on COR24?”

then the historically faithful model is:

front end: tokenizer/parser for RPG-II fixed-column specs

middle: build an internal representation of files, indicators, calculations, output specs, and the RPG cycle

back end: generate COR24 assembly or bytecode for a small runtime

runtime: implement the RPG cycle, record I/O, indicators, totals, overflow, and report formatting

That is much closer to how the original systems behaved than trying to build an AST interpreter first.

Best inference
I would not claim I found a single IBM source that explicitly says, word for word, “the RPG II compiler was written in assembler.” But given the period, the target hardware, and IBM’s tooling conventions, assembler is the overwhelmingly likely answer. 

For your COR24 emulator
You have three viable implementation styles:

Compiler to COR24 assembly
Most historically faithful. Best if you want “real language processor” feel.

Compiler to your p-code VM
Easier to get working. Good if you want RPG-II syntax and semantics without fighting COR24 register limits immediately.

Direct interpreter
Fastest prototype, but least like classic RPG-II systems.

For your setup, I would lean toward:

phase 1: parse RPG-II and execute via an interpreter for validation

phase 2: lower to a compact IR

phase 3: generate COR24 .s or p-code

That gives you correctness first, then faithfulness.

Card input/output
Use exact 80-byte fixed records.

Risks and tradeoffs
Risk: making it too interactive
If you lean too much into UART console behavior, you stop designing RPG-II and start inventing a general business shell language.

Mitigation
Keep console input as optional extension, not core path.

Risk: mixing printer and card semantics
Cards are record devices; printers are line devices.
If you unify them too aggressively, formatting gets messy.

Mitigation
Separate write_record and write_line.

Risk: overcommitting to native codegen too early
RPG-II semantics have enough quirks that direct codegen can slow you down.

Mitigation
Interpreter + IR first.
